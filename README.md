# Greek Economic Crisis: Debt Sustainability & Policy Failures

This repository presents an **econometric and theoretical analysis of the Greek Economic Crisis**, focusing on debt sustainability, fiscal mismanagement, expectations, and policy constraints within the Eurozone.

The project combines **economic theory, mathematical modeling, and VARX econometric analysis** to explain how Greece transitioned from growth to a sovereign debt crisis.

---

## 📌 Objectives
- Examine the **root causes** of the Greek debt crisis
- Apply **Debt Sustainability Theory**
- Analyze **macroeconomic determinants of Debt-to-GDP**
- Study the role of **expectations and investor confidence**
- Evaluate **policy failures and restricted solvency channels**

## 🧠 Theoretical Framework

The debt dynamics follow:

\[
d_{t+1} = (1+r_t)d_t + f_t
\]

\[
y_{t+1} = (1+g_t)y_t
\]

Debt sustainability depends on:
- Interest–growth differential \((r - g)\)
- Primary fiscal balance
- Initial debt stock

---

## 📈 Econometric Model

- **Model Used:** VARX(5)
- **Purpose:** Capture dynamic interactions between macroeconomic variables
- **Time Period:** Pre & post Global Financial Crisis

### Key Findings
- GDP growth impacts debt with **lagged effects**
- Unemployment significantly worsens debt dynamics
- Fiscal policy effects are **non-linear and delayed**
- FDI shows weak debt-reducing impact
- Eurozone membership constrained policy flexibility

---

## ⚠️ Limitations
- Multicollinearity
- Autocorrelation
- Heteroskedasticity
- Endogeneity concerns
- Lag length sensitivity

Future improvements include GLS, IV/2SLS, and optimal lag selection.

---

## 📉 Key Insights
- Greece suffered from **unsustainable debt dynamics**
- Austerity without growth deepened recession
- Loss of **market expectations** triggered liquidity crisis
- Absence of monetary independence worsened outcomes

---

## 🧾 Conclusion
The Greek crisis highlights how **fiscal mismanagement, weak institutions, and expectation shocks** can lead to sovereign collapse even within a monetary union. Sustainable recovery requires a balance of **fiscal discipline, growth policies, and institutional credibility**.
